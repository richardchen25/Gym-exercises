# Progression Guide

## How to progress within a level (double progression)

1. Pick a weight where you can hit the **bottom** of the rep range with good form.
2. Each session, try to add reps until you hit the **top** of the range on all sets.
3. Once you do, add weight (~2.5–5 lb upper body, ~5–10 lb lower body) and drop back to the bottom of the range.
4. Repeat.

## When to move up a level

**Beginner → Intermediate** (typically 4–6 months):
- You've been training consistently 3+ days/week for several months
- Machine weights have progressed steadily and now feel routine
- You're comfortable enough to learn barbell/dumbbell versions of the main movements

**Intermediate → Advanced** (typically 18–24+ months):
- Linear session-to-session progress has stalled despite good sleep, food, and effort
- Your technique on squat, bench, deadlift, and overhead press is consistent and confident
- You can gauge effort by RPE (see below) reasonably accurately

## RPE quick reference

| RPE | Meaning |
|---|---|
| 10 | Could not do another rep |
| 9 | 1 rep left in the tank |
| 8 | 2 reps left |
| 7 | 3 reps left |

## Deloads

Every 4–6 weeks of hard training (mainly intermediate/advanced), take an easy week: same movements, ~60–70% of your usual weight, fewer sets. You come back stronger.

## Plateau checklist

Before changing the program, check: sleep (7–9 hrs), protein (~0.7–1 g per lb bodyweight), calories (can't build much in a big deficit), and whether you're actually pushing sets close to failure.
